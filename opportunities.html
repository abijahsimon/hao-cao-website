Opportunities

Pathways for undergraduate researchers, graduate students, and postdoctoral scholars.

This page outlines opportunities for undergraduate student researchers, graduate student researchers, and postdoctoral scholars or assistant researchers. It gives prospective lab members a concise sense of who may be a natural fit and what kinds of projects connect to the group's work.

Undergraduate Student Researchers

Undergraduates with interests in physics, astronomy, planetary science, Earth and space sciences, applied mathematics, or scientific computing can be a strong fit for the group. Starter projects may include data analysis, coding for planetary field models, visualization, literature review, or foundational work tied to mission datasets. The strongest inquiries will briefly describe research interests, relevant coursework, and any programming or quantitative experience.

Graduate Student Researchers

Prospective graduate students interested in planetary magnetism, deep-interior dynamics, geophysical fluid dynamics, gravity and magnetic sounding, or spacecraft data interpretation should use this page as a starting point. The group spans observation, theory, and modeling, so students with different strengths can often find a way into the work if they are excited about planetary interiors and mission-driven science.

Postdoctoral Scholars and Assistant Researchers

Postdoctoral and research-staff opportunities are especially well matched to scientists who can lead or co-lead projects tied to mission datasets, dynamo modeling, magnetometer development, or the coupling between planetary interiors and space environments. The page is now structured to support future additions such as funded openings, fellowship pathways, or collaborations built around upcoming missions.

How to Reach Out

A good first email should include a short note on research interests, a CV or resume, and any relevant experience with coding, applied mathematics, data analysis, planetary science, or instrumentation. The contact workflow is intentionally simple so prospective researchers know where to begin.
