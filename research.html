Research

Hao Cao's research uses magnetic fields, gravity, fluid dynamics, and mission measurements to probe planetary interiors. The group connects spacecraft observations with theory, simulation, and instrument development to study the fluid motion, electrical structure, and dynamo processes that shape planets and moons.

01

Magnetic Fields of Giant Planets

A central thread in the group studies how Jupiter's and Saturn's magnetic fields are generated, how they evolve, and what they imply about electrically conducting regions deep below the visible atmosphere. Spacecraft magnetometry, especially from Juno and Cassini, is combined with dynamo modeling to connect external measurements to internal structure and fluid motion.

02

Gravitational Sounding of Deep Winds Inside Giant Planets

Jupiter's and Saturn's zonal winds leave signatures in the gravity field that can be measured remotely. This research direction asks how far the winds extend beneath the cloud tops, how their structure changes with depth, and how gravity data can be interpreted alongside magnetic constraints to understand giant-planet dynamics as a coupled system.

03

Gyroscopic Pumping and Geomagnetic Polar Minima

Although much of the group's work focuses on giant planets, Hao Cao's research also addresses core-flow processes relevant to Earth. Studies of geomagnetic polar minima investigate how fluid motion inside the core organizes the magnetic field and how theoretical ideas in rotating magnetohydrodynamics explain large-scale polar structure.

04

Zonal Flow Magnetic Field Interactions Inside Giant Planets

Deep atmospheric jets do not evolve in isolation. Where conducting regions are present, the flow and magnetic field influence each other, creating observable signatures that can be tested against spacecraft data. This theme connects fluid dynamics, magnetic induction, and inversion methods to understand how jets interact with the dynamo region inside giant planets.

05

A Dynamo Explanation for Mercury's North-South Asymmetric Magnetic Field

Mercury's magnetic field is unusually offset, with stronger structure in the north than the south. This line of work explores how dynamo processes operating in Mercury's core can produce that asymmetry, using Mercury as a compact but powerful case study for how interior dynamics shape planetary magnetism.
